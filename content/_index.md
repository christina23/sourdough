Sourdough is a perfect **background-day project** — most of the time it's just sitting there doing its thing while you go about your day. The active work is maybe 30 minutes total; the waiting is 7–8 hours. Plan it on a weekend when you're home anyway.

---

## What You Need

### Ingredients
- **450g bread flour** (or all-purpose; bread flour gives better structure)
- **325g water** (filtered or bottled — not tap; chlorine kills your starter)
- **9g salt**
- **90g active sourdough starter** (fed and bubbly, peaked or just past peak)

### Equipment
- Kitchen scale (volume measurements don't work well here)
- Large mixing bowl
- Dutch oven (5 qt is ideal)
- Parchment paper
- Banneton proofing basket, **or** a medium bowl + kitchen towel + rubber band

---

## Timeline

This is a same-day bake. Start in the morning and you'll have bread by dinner.

### ~0:00 — Feed your starter
If your starter has been in the fridge, take it out and feed it (equal parts starter, flour, water by weight). Wait until it's active and bubbly — usually 4–6 hours at room temp. If it's already active, skip ahead.

> **How to tell it's ready:** It should have roughly doubled, be domed or just starting to fall, and pass the float test (drop a small spoonful in water — it floats if active).

### ~0:30 — Mix the dough
Combine flour and water in a large bowl. Mix until no dry flour remains. Let it rest 30 minutes (this is **autolyse** — the flour hydrates and gluten starts forming without any effort from you).

After autolyse, add salt and starter. Squeeze and fold until fully incorporated. Cover and set a timer.

### ~1:00–3:00 — Stretch and fold
Do **4 sets of stretch and folds**, one every 30 minutes. Each set: grab one side of the dough, stretch it up, fold it over the center. Rotate the bowl and repeat 3 more times (4 folds per set = one set).

The dough will go from shaggy to smooth and elastic over these 2 hours.

### ~3:00–7:00 — Bulk fermentation
Leave the dough alone. Cover the bowl and let it ferment at room temperature for roughly 4 hours. This is the main rise.

> **Critical:** You're looking for ~50% volume increase, **not** double. Sourdough doesn't need to double during bulk — over-fermentation is one of the most common mistakes. The dough should look puffy, have some bubbles on the surface, and jiggle when you shake the bowl.

Temperature matters a lot here: a warmer kitchen (75–78°F) finishes bulk in 4 hours; a cooler kitchen (68–70°F) may need 5–6 hours.

### ~7:00 — Shape + second rise
Turn the dough out onto an unfloured surface. Do a pre-shape (pull it toward you a few times to build tension), rest 20 minutes, then do a final shape.

For the final shape:
1. Flip the dough, stretch it gently into a rectangle
2. Fold the sides in, then roll it toward you tightly
3. Flip seam-side down and use your hands to drag it toward you, building surface tension

Dust a banneton generously with rice flour (sticks less than all-purpose) and place the dough seam-side up. Alternatively: line a medium bowl with a floured kitchen towel, dust well, and place dough inside.

Put a rubber band around the outside of the bowl at the current dough level so you can track any rise. Cover with plastic wrap or a shower cap and refrigerate overnight — **or** let it proof at room temperature for 1–2 hours and bake same-day.

### ~8:00 — Score and bake

**Preheat** your oven to 450°F (230°C) with the dutch oven inside for at least 45 minutes.

When ready to bake:
1. Cut a piece of parchment to fit inside your dutch oven
2. Flip the dough out of the banneton onto the parchment
3. Score the top quickly with a lame or sharp knife — one confident slash at a 30–45° angle, about ½ inch deep
4. Lower into the hot dutch oven, put the lid on

**Bake covered 20 minutes**, then **remove the lid and bake 20 more minutes** until deep golden brown.

Let it cool on a rack for **at least 1 hour** before cutting. The interior is still cooking as it cools. Cutting early = gummy crumb.

## Tips & Gotchas

- **Don't use tap water.** Chlorine and chloramine in tap water can inhibit or kill your starter. Use filtered, bottled, or water that's been left out overnight to off-gas.

- **Watch the dough, not the clock.** The timeline above is a guide. Temperature, starter strength, and flour all affect timing. Learn to read the dough.

- **Reading your crumb:** An open, irregular crumb (big holes) = well-fermented, well-developed dough. A tight, uniform crumb = under-fermented or under-developed. A gummy or dense crumb = either under-baked or cut too early. See the [r/sourdough fermentation crumb guide](https://www.reddit.com/r/Sourdough/comments/1b0t5jr/reading_crumb_for_fermentation/) for photos and analysis.

- **Starter readiness matters more than anything.** If your starter isn't active, the bread won't rise. When in doubt, wait for the starter.

- **Cold proof = more flavor, more flexibility.** An overnight cold proof in the fridge develops more sour flavor and gives you flexibility on when to bake. You can bake straight from the fridge — no need to come to room temp first.

- **The dutch oven is doing a lot of work.** The lid traps steam, which keeps the crust soft enough to expand in the first 20 minutes (oven spring). Without it, the crust sets too fast and the bread can't fully open.
