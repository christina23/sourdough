A personal guide based on [Amy Duska's recipes at Little Spoon Farm](https://littlespoonfarm.com). Sourdough is a background-day project — most of the time it's just sitting there doing its thing. The active work is maybe 30 minutes total; the waiting is days (for your starter) then hours (for your bread).

## Part 1: Creating Your Starter

*Based on Amy Duska's [Beginner Sourdough Starter Recipe](https://littlespoonfarm.com/sourdough-starter-recipe/)*

A sourdough starter is a live culture of wild yeast and bacteria that you cultivate from scratch. It takes 7–10 days to establish. Once it's active, you keep it alive with regular feedings and it lasts indefinitely.

### What You Need

**Flour mix (make this first and store in an airtight container):**
- 6 cups (720g) organic all-purpose flour
- 3 cups (360g) organic whole wheat flour

This blend is what you'll use for all feedings. The whole wheat gives wild yeast something extra to grab onto early on.

**Equipment:** Glass jar, kitchen scale, offset spatula or spoon

> **Use filtered or bottled water throughout.** Chlorine and chloramine in tap water can inhibit the wild yeast you're trying to cultivate.

### The 7-Day Schedule

**Day 1**
Add 1 cup of the flour mix (120g) and ½ cup of water (120g) to a clean jar. Stir thoroughly until no dry flour remains. Cover with a lid or breathable cloth secured with a rubber band. Place in a warm spot — around 75°F (24°C) — for 24 hours.

**Day 2**
Give the starter a stir to incorporate a little air. Cover and let it rest another 24 hours in a warm spot. You may see a few small bubbles — that's a good sign.

**Days 3–7 (daily)**
Every 24 hours:
1. Discard all but 2 tablespoons of the starter
2. Feed with ½ cup fresh flour mix (60g) + ¼ cup water (60g)
3. Stir thoroughly, cover, and return to its warm spot

Repeat this daily until your starter is active, bubbly, and **doubles in size within 4–12 hours of feeding**. This typically takes 7–10 days.

> **Sluggish starter?** If it seems slow after Day 4, try giving it a mid-cycle boost: 12 hours after the regular feeding, stir in 2 tablespoons of flour + 2 teaspoons of water. This helps it along until the next full feeding.

### How to Know It's Ready

Your starter is ready to bake with when it:
- Doubles in size within 4–12 hours of a feeding
- Has plenty of bubbles throughout
- Smells tangy and slightly yeasty (not putrid)
- Passes the **float test**: drop a small spoonful into a glass of water — if it floats, it's active

### After Day 7

Keep feeding daily for the first two weeks to continue building up the yeast and bacteria. This gives you a robust, mature starter that will produce better bread. After that, you can move it to the fridge and feed weekly (see Part 3).

## Part 2: Baking Your First Loaf

*Based on Amy Duska's [Easy Sourdough Bread Recipe](https://littlespoonfarm.com/sourdough-bread-recipe-beginners-guide/)*

This is a same-day bake. Start in the morning and you'll have bread by evening.

### Ingredients

**Prepare your starter 12 hours before mixing dough:**
- 2 tsp sourdough starter
- 3 tbsp all-purpose flour
- 5 tsp water

This small build creates about ¼ cup of fresh active starter for your loaf.

**For the dough:**
- 50g active sourdough starter
- 350g water
- 10g fine sea salt
- 500g bread flour

> **Equipment:** Kitchen scale, large mixing bowl, Dutch oven (5 qt), parchment paper, banneton or towel-lined bowl

### Steps

**12 hours before — Feed your starter**
Mix the starter build ingredients in a clean jar. It's ready when it has doubled and is bubbly. Time this so it peaks when you're ready to mix dough.

**Step 1 — Mix the dough**
Combine 50g active starter with 350g water in a large bowl, stirring to distribute evenly. Add 500g bread flour and 10g salt. Work together until a shaggy mass forms with no dry flour remaining. Cover and rest 1 hour at room temperature.

**Step 2 — Stretch and fold (2 rounds)**
Wet your hand to prevent sticking. Grab one side of the dough, stretch it up, and fold it over itself. Rotate the bowl a quarter turn and repeat until you've made a full circle — that's one set. Cover and rest 30 minutes. Do one more full set, then cover.

**Step 3 — Bulk fermentation**
Cover the bowl and leave the dough on the counter for **7–10 hours**. It will rise about 50–75% — not fully double. The dough should look puffy, have bubbles on the surface, and jiggle when you shake the bowl.

> **Over-proofed dough becomes sticky and difficult to shape.** In a warm kitchen (above 76°F), check it earlier.

**Step 4 — Shape and second rise**
Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Pull the four sides into the middle, then flip seam-side down. Cup the dough and pull it gently toward you, rotating, to build a tight skin on the surface.

Place on parchment paper, seam-side down, in a medium bowl. Cover with a tea towel and rest 1–2 hours at room temperature.

> **Poke test:** Press your thumb ½ inch into the dough. If it springs back all the way, it needs more time. If it springs back slowly and holds a slight indent, it's ready.

**Option: Cold proof overnight (2-day method)**
Instead of the room-temperature rise, you can refrigerate the shaped dough overnight. This is a great option if you want fresher bread in the morning, more flexibility on timing, or a more complex sour flavor.

After shaping, place the dough seam-side up in a well-floured banneton (or a towel-lined bowl dusted with rice flour). Cover tightly with plastic wrap and refrigerate for **12–48 hours** — 24 hours is the sweet spot. The fridge slows fermentation way down, so it won't over-proof the way it would sitting on the counter.

When ready to bake: remove from the fridge, turn it out onto parchment paper seam-side down, and let it rest at room temperature for **2 hours** before scoring and baking. Then proceed to Step 5 as normal.

**Step 5 — Score and bake**
Preheat oven to 450°F (232°C) with the Dutch oven inside for at least 30 minutes.

1. Score the top of the dough with a sharp knife or lame — one confident slash at a 30–45° angle, about ½ inch deep
2. Use the parchment as a sling to lower the dough into the hot Dutch oven
3. Bake **covered for 20 minutes**, then **uncovered for 25–30 minutes** until deeply golden brown
4. Internal temperature should reach 205–210°F (96–99°C)

**Step 6 — Cool**
Transfer to a wire rack and cool for **1–2 hours** before slicing. The bread continues to cook internally as it cools. Cutting early = gummy crumb.

### Tips

- **All-purpose instead of bread flour?** Reduce water to 325g.
- **Two loaves:** Divide dough equally before shaping. Bake 20 min covered, 15–20 min uncovered.
- **Less sour flavor:** Feed your starter more frequently in the days before baking.
- **Storage:** Room temperature in a bread bag or under a cake dome. Do not refrigerate — it stales faster. Freeze (wrapped tightly) for up to 3 months.

## Part 3: Tending to Your Starter

*Based on Amy Duska's [How to Feed & Maintain a Sourdough Starter](https://littlespoonfarm.com/how-to-feed-maintain-sourdough-starter/)*

Once established, a sourdough starter is surprisingly resilient — but it needs consistent care. Here's how to keep it healthy for the long haul.

### Why You Discard

Before every feeding, you discard most of your starter. This isn't wasteful — it's essential. Discarding removes old, acidic culture that would eventually overwhelm the yeast, keeps the quantity manageable, and makes room for fresh flour and water. The discarded portion can be used in pancakes, crackers, waffles, and other discard recipes.

### Feeding Ratios

Starter is fed using a **starter : flour : water** ratio by weight.

| Ratio | Example | When to use |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **1:1:1** | 50g starter + 50g flour + 50g water | Daily bakers; peaks in 4–6 hours |
| **1:4:4** | 25g starter + 100g flour + 100g water | Slower rise; longer window before peak |

Use the 1:1:1 ratio for regular maintenance. Use 1:4:4 when you need the starter to peak later in the day, or when your kitchen is warm and you want to slow things down.

### If You Bake Weekly: The Fridge Method

1. Feed your starter at your normal ratio
2. Let it sit at room temperature for 1–2 hours (so it has something to eat)
3. Place it in the refrigerator
4. Once a week, take it out, discard, feed, let it sit 1–2 hours, then return to the fridge
5. Before baking, take it out and feed it 1–2 times at room temperature until it's active and doubling reliably

### Signs Your Starter Is Healthy

- Doubles within 4–12 hours of feeding
- Bubbly throughout with a domed top at peak
- Smells tangy and yeasty — pleasant sour, not rotten
- Has a consistent rhythm: rises and falls predictably

### Troubleshooting

**Hooch (liquid on top)**
A dark, grayish liquid pooling on top means your starter is hungry — it's eaten through its food and is producing alcohol. You can stir it back in (makes the bread more sour) or pour it off before feeding. If hooch keeps appearing between feedings, either feed more frequently or increase the flour-to-starter ratio (try 1:2:2 or 1:4:4).

**No activity after Day 5**
Try the mid-cycle boost: stir in 2 tablespoons of flour + 2 teaspoons of water halfway between feedings. Also check the temperature — wild yeast is sluggish below 70°F. Move the jar somewhere warmer (top of the fridge, near the oven, or inside the oven with just the light on).

**Starter smells like nail polish remover or acetone**
This is a sign of too much acetic acid — the starter is very hungry and over-acidified. Discard aggressively (keep just 1 tablespoon) and do 1–2 feedings in quick succession with fresh flour and water. It should recover within a day or two.

**Pink or orange streaks**
Discard it and start over. This indicates contamination with unwanted bacteria. Clean the jar thoroughly before starting fresh.

**Forgotten in the fridge for weeks (or months)**
Don't throw it away. Take it out, let it come to room temperature, discard down to a tablespoon, and feed it. Repeat daily for 3–5 days. Most starters recover fully even after long neglect.
